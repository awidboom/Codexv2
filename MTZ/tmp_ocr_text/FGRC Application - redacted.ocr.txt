APPLICATIONSUMMARY
This application is for replacement of its flare gas recovery compressors that serve as
abatement devices (A-13 and A-26) located at the Valero Benicia Refinery (Valero) with
two more reliable compressors. Valero plans to install modern rotary screw-type
compressors in place of the current older units which are reaching end of useful life. The
replacement compressor capacity will have the same capacity as the existing compressors
of 6 million cubic feet/ day each. Overall facility emissions will be reduced as a result of
replacing the existing compressors with modern, more reliable compressors. Valero
respectfully requests action by the District to process this permit application under the
Accelerated Permitting Program, with consideration by the District that this project may
qualify as exempt from permitting pursuant to Regulation 2, Rule 1, Section 2-1-128.21.
Section 2.0 provides more details on the proposed project. This is followed by a summary
of the impact on emissions in Section 3.0. A regulatory applicability discussion is
provided in Section 4.0, and proposed permit conditions are included in Section 5.0.
Applicable fees will be paid as stated in Section 6.0. Appendix A contains the required
compressors.
2.0
PROJECT DESCRIPTION
Valero seeks to replace the two flare gas recovery compressors (A-13 and A-26), with the
objective of improving reliability and added benefit of reducing emissions. The existing
compressors designated as C-2101 A and B are reciprocating compressors, each having a
rating of 6 million cubic feet/day. C-2101A was installed in 1975 and C-2101B was
installed in 1983. While it is possible to operate both compressors at the same time, under
normal conditions, one serves as a primary unit and the other as a backup.
Valero is planning to install two replacement flare gas compressors, which are new
rotary screw-type compressors that meet BACT standards and are more reliable than the
existing reciprocating compressors. The new compressors will be operated similarly to C-
2101 A and B, with one operating as the primary compressor and the other available as a
spare unit. C-2101B will be replaced first. After C-2101B is replaced, C-2101A will be
replaced. This replacement plan will ensure there is at least one flare gas compressor
available at all times to recover vent gas.
The two compressors effectively serve to minimize emissions from the flares. Under
normal conditions, gases from the process unit vents, purges, tankage, vessels and relief
systems are routed to the flare gas header and compressors for recovery. Use of the flare
is limited to circumstances where flaring cannot be eliminated despite careful planning
and prevention actions. Figure 1 shows the location of the compressors and Figure 2
shows a simplified process flow diagram of the Flare Gas Recovery System where the
replacement compressors would be located downstream of the Flare Gas Header.

EMISSIONS
Overall, facility emissions will be reduced as a result of replacing the existing
compressors with more reliable compressors. These compressors serve to recover gas
that would otherwise be flared. Examples of events (listed in the Benicia Refinery Flare
maintenance, process unit startup, shutdown, and turnaround events;
fuel gas quantity and quality issues such as fuel gas imbalanced caused by high
nitrogenorhydrogencontent;

With new compressors that are more reliable and efficient, recovery of more gases during
some of the above events will be possible. While these gases, once recovered, will still be
combusted as fuel for refinery equipment (e.g., heaters, boilers), there are several benefits
of recovery versus flaring that would reduce emissions including:
recoveredgases aretreatedtoremovesulfurthatwouldnototherwisebe
removed if sent to the flares.
additional recovered gas sent to the fuel gas unit will reduce some of the demand
for make-up fuel (natural gas), thus reducing energy demand; and
recoveredgasesused asfuelwill beburnedundermorecontrolled conditions at
sources such as heaters and boilers, rather than burning the gases at flares (S-18
and S-19).
As with the existing compressors, the new compressors would only be a source of
fugitive emissions. The fugitive emissions of the new compressors compared to the
existing compressors will decrease due to 1) the replacement of old equipment with new
modern BACT-compliant compressors and 2) the decrease of fugitive components on the
new compressors.The emissions estimate below only takes into account the emissions
decrease due to #2, decrease of fugitive component count, and not the emissions decrease
due to installing modern BACT-compliant compressors.

direct result of the use of an abatement device to comply with BACT requirements for

The BACT Workbook Guideline specifies BACT requirements for common permitted
sources and presents two BACT levels of control: 1) Technologically Feasible/Cost
Effective and 2) Achieved in practice (level 2 being the minimum required BACT). The
BACT Workbook at a minimum specifies BACT level 2 for compressors achieve
emissions less than 500 ppm expressed as methane measured using EPA Method 21. The
BACT Workbook identifies the typical technology to achieve BACT level 2 is "Double
Mechanical seals w/ Barrier Fluid, and BAAQMD Approved Quarterly Inspection and
Maintenance Program." The new compressors will be designed such that at a minimum
they will comply with the BACT level 2.
