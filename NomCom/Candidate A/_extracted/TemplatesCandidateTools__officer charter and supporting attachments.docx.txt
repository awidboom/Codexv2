officer charter and supporting attachments
An officer is a company leader who is committed to supporting the company culture; leading company business, functions, and initiatives; and appropriately representing and committing the company and participating in company management. Barr relies on officers to behave ethically; employ good judgment in balancing company, staff, and client concerns; and model a willingness to put the long-term success of Barr Engineering Co. first. An officer assesses and manages risks and uncertainty while executing their leadership role within the boundaries laid out in our role descriptions and business plans and, where appropriate, in consultation with their fellow officers.
A Barr officer is expected to comply with this charter continuously during their term of office. In accordance with the Conformed Bylaws of Barr Engineering Co., an officer is appointed by the board of directors and serves in the role until the officer resigns or is removed from the role by the board of directors.
an officer’s rights and responsibilities
The primary right conferred on a Barr officer is the right to officially represent or commit the company (in whole or part) and its resources (staff, cash, equipment, facilities, intellectual property, brand, and other assets). Externally, these representations and commitments may be made by executing a contract on Barr’s behalf but can take other forms such as proposals, negotiations, positions on issues, and verbal representations. Because of the weight that external stakeholders may place on the title, officers of Barr have an important responsibility to reflect positively on the reputation and character of the Company, and to carefully consider the representations they make and how those might be interpreted as official commitments or positions. Internally, these representations and commitments are made through accepting a leadership role (president, business support vice president, and business unit vice president) and agreeing to and performing the tasks and responsibilities listed in the corresponding role description. All officers are responsible for risk management, financial stewardship, safety, professional ethics, maintaining a respectful workplace, staff development, and participation in company leadership. While responsibility ultimately lies with the officer, officers may delegate day-to-day responsibilities.
Management team members have an important responsibility and authority to monitor officers assigned to their subunits and take appropriate coaching or other actions on behalf of the company to ensure officer performance is consistent with this charter and the applicable role description. Officers are accountable (expected to be able to justify their actions and decisions) to their business partners: other officers, the management team and CEO, and ultimately Barr’s board of directors and shareholders.
expectations for an officer
An officer is expected to demonstrate the following attributes and competencies and continually hone their skills and effectiveness in these areas:
Embraces Barr’s values, culture, strategies, and consulting and business models and contributes to constructive change
Exercises good judgment and courage
Provides strategic thinking and leadership in area of practice or responsibility (including influencing, delegating, holding themselves and others accountable)
Helps develop talent and helps colleagues reach full potential
Developing and maintaining strong internal and external relationships
Providing quality work and exceptional client service
Contributing to the success of the company as a whole, beyond their functional domain

Attachment B
vice president selection criteria
Below are listed the specific vice president role selection criteria that will be used to guide development and appointment of vice president officers. The process for developing and appointing business support group vice presidents and business unit vice presidents are described in company operations practice documents nos. 301 and 302, respectively. In addition to a description of the criteria we have listed qualitative evidence that candidates and evaluators need to consider to assess whether the criteria have been achieved. We have also listed suggested metrics and other means of assessment that could be considered by candidates and evaluators. These terms are further defined as follows:
Criteria – the criteria are the key experiences, competencies and attributes that must be demonstrated by every individual to be appointed to the role. These criteria may be met through different channels, depending on the candidate and their area of business.
Qualitative evidence – a preponderance of this list of qualitative evidence must be witnessed or verified by evaluators (other officers, board directors, management team members). The candidate may not have had the opportunity to demonstrate every one of the attributes described in these lists, but they cannot have consistently demonstrated behaviors opposing them. They can be working on some of the attributes if they are showing good progress in their understanding of their benefit.
Potential basis for assessment/metrics – Evaluators may consider these assessment approaches and specific metrics suggested as they consider candidates. We believe periodically collecting this type of information centrally and providing consistent information to candidates and evaluators is essential. We do not suggest providing bright lines or thresholds with this information because applying this information to each individual is unique and situational.
