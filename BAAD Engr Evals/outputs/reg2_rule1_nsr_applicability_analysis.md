# Regulation 2, Rule 1 � NSR Applicability Analysis

## Core point

A `Regulation 2, Rule 1` NSR applicability analysis is primarily a **permitability and classification** analysis. `Rule 1` answers the threshold questions:

1. is the equipment a `source`;
2. is it `new`, `altered`, or `modified`;
3. is an `Authority to Construct` and later a `Permit to Operate` required under `2-1-301` and `2-1-302`;
4. does an exemption apply under `2-1-103`, `2-1-105`, or `2-1-113` through `2-1-128`;
5. even if a facial exemption applies, is that exemption overridden by `2-1-316` through `2-1-319`;
6. if a permit is required, what other Regulation 2 overlays now apply, especially `Rule 2` for NSR and `Rule 5` for toxics.

So the clean way to think about it is:

- `Rule 1` is the **gatekeeper** for whether the project enters the permitting program at all;
- `Rule 2` supplies the core NSR substantive requirements once the project is a permitted `new` or `modified` source;
- `Rule 5` adds the toxics-specific overlay for projects involving TACs.

## Rule excerpts that drive the threshold analysis

### Purpose of Rule 1

> `2-1-101`: "The purpose of Regulation 2 is to provide an orderly procedure for the review of new sources of air pollution, and of the modification and operation of existing sources ... through the issuance of authorities to construct and permits to operate."

Source: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:27`

### Key defined terms

> `2-1-221 Source`: "Any article, machine, equipment, operation, contrivance or related groupings of such which may produce and/or emit air pollutants."

> `2-1-217 Potential to Emit`: "The maximum capacity of a source or facility to emit a pollutant based on its physical and operational design," taking enforceable limits into account.

> `2-1-232 New Source`: includes a source never previously existing or permitted, a source out of service for one year without a valid PTO, a relocation to a non-contiguous property, and a replacement source.

> `2-1-233 Alter`: a physical or operational change that may affect emissions but does not qualify as a modification.

> `2-1-234 Modify`: a physical, operational, throughput, production, or similar change that results in an emissions increase, including an increase in `potential to emit` or a federal `major modification` increase.

Sources: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1133`, `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1153`, `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1261`, `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1293`, `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1301`

### Basic permit requirements

> `2-1-301`: any person who builds, installs, modifies, modernizes, alters, or replaces equipment whose use may cause, reduce, or control emissions must first secure an `Authority to Construct`.

> `2-1-302`: before using or operating such equipment, the person must secure a `Permit to Operate`.

Source: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1419`

### Toxics and other exemption overrides

> `2-1-316`: notwithstanding exemptions in `2-1-103` and `2-1-114` through `2-1-128`, a new or modified source meeting the toxics / HAP thresholds is subject to `2-1-301` and/or `2-1-302`.

> `2-1-317`: a source can lose exempt treatment if it becomes a public nuisance source.

> `2-1-318`: certain hazardous-substance thresholds override exemptions.

> `2-1-319`: sources above specified emission levels are expressly subject to PTO requirements.

Sources: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1709`, `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1733`, `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1741`, `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1781`

## Information request

Before analyzing NSR applicability under `Rule 1`, assemble at least the following:

- a complete list of affected equipment and activities;
- identification of which units are:
  - new;
  - relocated;
  - replacement sources;
  - undergoing a physical change;
  - undergoing an operational change;
  - undergoing a throughput or production change;
- a description of how each unit operates now and how it will operate after the project, including raw materials, fuels, throughput, schedules, batch frequency, control-device use, startup/shutdown patterns, and any other operational details needed to test the permit exemptions in `2-1-103` and `2-1-113` through `2-1-128`;
- current permit status for each affected source, including existing `ATC`, `PTO`, `Title V`, source test requirements, and all permit-condition limits on emissions, throughput, hours of operation, material usage, sulfur content, control efficiency, and monitoring / recordkeeping;
- enough information to establish **existing PTE** for each existing source and pollutant, including:
  - the source's physical design capacity;
  - current permitted limits and whether they are federally / District enforceable;
  - actual operational bottlenecks upstream and downstream;
  - control equipment and whether associated limits are enforceable;
  - any exceeded permit limits, because an exceeded enforceable limit does not constrain `PTE` under `2-1-217`;
  - any prior District representations or engineering assumptions that were used to establish source capacity;
  - for sources with firm or `NSR` limits, the existing limits plus enough design / engineering information to show the limits are actually achievable and not defeated by bottlenecks;
  - for grandfathered or otherwise non-firm-limited sources, historical daily and annual operating data far enough back to identify the highest achieved daily and annual rates that BAAD has sometimes used in practice to define pre-project `PTE`;
  - where multiple materials or operating scenarios are possible, scenario-specific information sufficient to identify the highest-emitting case;
- current and post-project `PTE` for each pollutant that may matter for `Rule 1`, `Rule 2`, and `Rule 5`;
- for existing sources, baseline actual emissions data needed for any federal major-modification analysis, including monthly emissions and operating data sufficient to identify the representative `24-month` baseline period within the last `10 years` for each pollutant, using the same `24-month` period for all sources in the project for a given pollutant;
- for existing sources, baseline actual emissions data needed for other Rule 2 calculations, including the information needed to evaluate the applicable baseline methodology if the project is not using the federal major-modification test;
- identification of all emitted TACs and HAPs;
- daily and annual emissions estimates, including emissions after control and, where required, without credit for abatement;
- whether the source category is subject to another District rule, especially `Regulation 6`, `Regulation 8` (other than Rules 1 through 4), or `Regulations 9` through `12`;
- facility location relative to schools and whether the project is in an `Overburdened Community`;
- whether the project is at a facility with prior related permit actions that could affect cumulative or project-level review.

These inputs are needed because `Rule 1` depends heavily on source classification, permit status, exemption status, emissions levels, and the actual way the unit is operated.

## Recommended decision sequence

### 1. Determine whether the affected item is a `source`

Start with `2-1-221`. If the item is an article, machine, equipment, operation, contrivance, or related grouping that may emit air pollutants, treat it as a `source` unless there is a clear exclusion elsewhere.

This step matters because the rest of `Rule 1` is written around whether a `source` is new, altered, modified, exempt, or expressly subject to permitting.

### 2. Check facial exemptions under `Rule 1`

The first exemption screen is whether the source is exempt under:

- `2-1-103` source not subject to any District rule;
- `2-1-105` registered statewide portable equipment;
- `2-1-113` through `2-1-128` miscellaneous source and operation exemptions.

For example, `2-1-103` exempts a source only if it is not in specified source categories, is not subject to `2-1-316` through `319`, and stays below listed daily / annual emissions criteria.

> `2-1-103.2`: the source must not be subject to `2-1-316` through `319`.

> `2-1-103.3`: emissions of listed criteria pollutants must be below the stated thresholds.

Source: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:43`

So the exemption analysis is never just ?does it fit a listed exempt source category.? It also requires checking how the unit actually operates against the exemption language.

### 3. Decide whether the project is a `new source`, `alteration`, or `modification`

This is the central classification step.

- Use `2-1-232` for `new source`.
- Use `2-1-233` for `alteration`.
- Use `2-1-234` for `modification`.

A `modification` analysis under `Rule 1` usually requires emissions work, because `2-1-234` turns on whether the change results in an emissions increase. For many projects this means calculating pre-project and post-project `PTE`, and in some cases also evaluating federal major-modification concepts incorporated through `2-1-234.2`.

For federal major-modification work, baseline actual emissions may require identifying the representative `24-month` period within the last `10 years` for each pollutant for the federal categories that use that framework, using the same `24-month` period for all sources in the project for a given pollutant.

As a practical BAAD point, for existing units you should compare pre-project and post-project `PTE` on a unit-by-unit basis and consider whether alternate enforceable limits could be taken to avoid an increase. For grandfathered units, that can effectively mean "baselining" the unit by taking limits equivalent to what has actually been achieved historically, subject to the operational consequences of those restrictions.

A critical detail in `2-1-234.1.3` is that for `TACs` and `HAPs`, a change is not a `modification` unless the increase in `PTE` results in a cancer-risk increase greater than `1 in a million` or a chronic hazard-index increase greater than `0.20`; increases below `Table 2-5-1` are presumed not to exceed those risk levels.

Sources: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1301`, `NSR guidance/_text/Reg 2 NSR Permitting Presentation_Sept 30 2016 pdf.txt:425`

### 4. Check whether exemptions are overridden by `2-1-316` through `2-1-319`

This is where many `Rule 1` analyses become actual NSR permitting analyses.

A practical point from `Barr Approach.docx` is that even where a unit is not expected to be `modified` under the simple `PTE` comparison, you should still consider whether it needs to be carried forward into the federal backstop analysis for consistency with the broader project review.

#### `2-1-316` ? toxics / HAP override

This section is more specific than just saying ?toxics / HAP thresholds.? It does two different things:

- `2-1-316.1`: if a **new or modified source** emits one or more `TACs` in quantities that exceed the trigger levels in `Rule 2-5 Table 2-5-1`, and the source did not have a valid exemption from `2-1-302` when the source was constructed or modified, then the source becomes subject to `2-1-301` and `2-1-302` unless the owner/operator can demonstrate to the APCO's satisfaction that the source will comply with `2-5-301` `TBACT` and `2-5-302` project risk limits, if applicable.
- `2-1-316.2`: if a **new or modified source**, or a **group of related sources** in a proposed construction or modification, will emit `2.5 tons/year` or more of any single `HAP`, or `6.25 tons/year` or more of any combination of `HAPs`, then it is subject to `2-1-301` and `2-1-302`.

So the key comparisons under `2-1-316` are:
- source `TAC` emissions versus `Table 2-5-1` trigger levels;
- single-`HAP` emissions versus `2.5 tpy`;
- combined `HAP` emissions versus `6.25 tpy`.

This is the main point where `Rule 1` and `Rule 5` directly intersect.

#### `2-1-317` ? public nuisance override

An otherwise exempt source can be pulled into permitting if it receives repeated public nuisance violations.

#### `2-1-318` ? hazardous substances override

Certain hazardous-substance emission thresholds at PSD-scale facilities override exemptions.

#### `2-1-319` ? express permitting threshold

A source is expressly subject to `PTO` requirements if either:
- emissions of any **regulated air pollutant**, except greenhouse gases, are greater than `5 tpy` after abatement; or
- the source is subject to `2-1-316`, `2-1-317`, or `2-1-318`.

That is broader than just criteria pollutants. `2-1-218` defines a `regulated air pollutant` as any air pollutant that is subject to a regulation, so the `2-1-319` screen should be treated as a broad regulated-pollutant review, not a narrow criteria-pollutant test.

Sources: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1133`, `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1709`, `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1781`

### 5. Determine whether accelerated permitting is available

Before moving into the substantive `Rule 2` NSR review, determine whether the project qualifies for the temporary-permit / accelerated-permitting pathway in `2-1-302.2` and `2-1-106`.

This should be treated as a discrete decision point, not just an administrative afterthought, because it affects the immediate permitting path and whether the project can begin construction or operation under a temporary `PTO` pending full review.

The key questions are:
- is the source excluded because it is subject to `2-1-316` through `2-1-319`;
- is it a qualifying new source, modification, replacement abatement device, or alteration;
- are the qualifying low-emissions criteria satisfied;
- is the source outside the `2-1-412` public-notice trigger.

If the project does not qualify, continue with the ordinary permitting path.

Source: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1427`, `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:43`

### 6. Perform the federal backstop test under `2-1-234.2`, where applicable

If there are new, modified, or altered units, perform the separate federal-backstop-style applicability review incorporated through `2-1-234.2`.

In practical terms, this means completing a traditional PSD / major-modification evaluation using the incorporated federal framework, including the analogous federal nonattainment concepts where applicable. A useful implementation point from `Barr Approach.docx` is to plan to include altered units in this step for consistency, even where you are not independently expecting an emissions increase from those units.

If this analysis shows PSD or major-modification consequences, additional `Rule 2` review is required and BAAD interpretation may become important on how altered units are treated within the broader project.

Sources: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:1301`, `NSR guidance/_text/Reg 2 NSR Permitting Presentation_Sept 30 2016 pdf.txt:337`

### 7. If a permit is required, determine what substantive NSR review applies under `Rule 2`

`Rule 1` gets the project into the permitting program. `Rule 2` then supplies the substantive NSR requirements.

> `Rule 2-2-101`: `Rule 2` applies to all new and modified sources that are subject to `2-1-301` and/or `2-1-302`.

Source: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-2.md:27`

For the detailed `Rule 2` NSR analysis, use a separate memo to be developed later.

### 8. Determine whether `Rule 5` adds a toxics overlay

If the project has `TAC` emissions, `Rule 5` may apply in addition to `Rule 1` and `Rule 2`.

For the detailed toxics applicability and `Rule 5` analysis, refer to `outputs/reg2_rule5_toxics_applicability_analysis.md:1`.

### 9. Check public notice and permitting path details

Even after permit applicability is established, `Rule 1` still controls key administrative consequences.

#### Public notice

`2-1-412` requires public notice for:
- new or modified sources within `1000 feet` of a `K-12` schoolsite with increases in listed toxic or hazardous substances; and
- new or modified sources in an `Overburdened Community` where an `HRA` is required under `2-5-401`.

Source: `baaqmd_rules/rag_md/rule-regulation-2-rule-1.md:2031`

## Practical checklist

A defensible `Rule 1` NSR applicability analysis usually answers these questions in order:

1. What are all the affected `sources`?
2. Which are `new`, `altered`, or `modified`?
3. What are current and post-project emissions / `PTE`?
4. Does `2-1-301` / `302` apply by default?
5. Does a facial exemption apply?
6. Is that exemption overridden by `2-1-316`, `317`, `318`, or `319`?
7. If a permit is required, which `Rule 2` substantive NSR requirements apply?
8. If TACs are involved, which `Rule 5` requirements apply?
9. Is public notice required under `2-1-412`?
10. Is the project eligible for accelerated processing or ordinary processing?
11. Is the project eligible for ministerial or discretionary treatment?

## Supporting guidance

The `MOP Vol. II, Part 4` guidance is consistent with this structure. It states that permit applications are required for new and modified TAC-emitting sources that are subject to `2-1-301` / `302`, and that a permit is not required only if the source is exempt, has no TAC emissions above trigger levels, and is not otherwise subject to `2-1-316.2`, `317`, `318`, or `319`.

Source: `NSR guidance/_text/vol2_pt4.txt:286`

It also states that permits may still be required for sources that would otherwise be exempt if they exceed `Table 2-5-1` trigger levels, and that the District may require the owner/operator to demonstrate compliance with `2-1-316.1` and `316.2`.

Source: `NSR guidance/_text/vol2_pt4.txt:299`

## Bottom line

The right way to do a `Regulation 2, Rule 1` NSR applicability analysis is to treat it as a structured gateway review:

- classify the source;
- determine the default permit obligation;
- test every claimed exemption;
- test every exemption override;
- then hand the project to `Rule 2` and `Rule 5` for substantive NSR review.

If you skip the override step in `2-1-316` through `319`, or if you jump straight to `Rule 2` without first resolving the `Rule 1` permit question, the analysis is incomplete.
