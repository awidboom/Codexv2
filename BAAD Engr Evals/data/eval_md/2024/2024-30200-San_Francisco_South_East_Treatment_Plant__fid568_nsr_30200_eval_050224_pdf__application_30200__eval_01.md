BACKGROUND
The San Francisco South East Treatment Plant has applied for an Authority to Construct and/or 
Permit to Operate for the following: 
S-(110) Pretreatment Operations [existing] 
abated by 
A-(110) Biofiltration Odor Control, four vessels [new] 
91,000 cfm to A-111 in normal mode, 60,667 cfm to A-111 and 30,333 cfm to 
atmosphere in bypass mode, 91,000 cfm to atmosphere in alternative normal 
mode 
and 
A-(111) Carbon Adsorption Odor Control, three vessels [new]  
91,000 cfm to atmosphere in normal mode, 60,667 in bypass mode 
In normal mode, A-110 and A-111 are in series - 91,000 cfm will pass through A-110 before 
exhausting from A-111. In bypass mode, the flow rate will stay the same, but 30,333 cfm will 
exhaust from A-110 and the remaining 60,667 cfm will exhaust from A-111. In alternative 
normal mode, 91,000 cfm will exhaust directly from A-110. 
The following abatement devices will be removed: 
A-(1204) Biofilter, 2,500 cfm 
A-(1205) Biofilter, 2,500 cfm 
And an unpermitted dual (dry) media scrubber, 18,000 cfm 
The sources above will be located at 750 Phelps Street, San Francisco, CA 94124. 
The San Francisco South East Treatment Plant (SEP), owns and operates a municipal wastewater 
treatment plant. This plant was constructed in 1952 and handles about 80% of San Francisco’s 
wastewater from the eastern two thirds of the city’s residents. SEP’s peak wet weather flow rate 
is approximately 250 million gallons per day (MGD); 150 MGD receives primary and secondary 
treatment and the additional 100 MGD receives only primary treatment. The permitted average 
dry weather flow rate is 85.4 million gallons per day. This project will permit the new 
preliminary treatment facility (SEP 020 Headworks), which will replace the existing headworks 
buildings SEP 011 (wet weather headworks) and SEP 012 (all weather headworks) under Source 
110, Preliminary Treatment.

The HRA estimates residential risk assuming exposure to annual average TAC concentrations 
occurring 350 days per year, for 30 years. Risk estimate for offsite workers assumes an exposure 
that occurs 8 hours per day, 250 days per year, for 25 years. 
 
Results from this HRA indicate that the maximum project cancer risk is estimated at 0.90 in a 
million, that the project chronic hazard index is 0.086, and that the project acute health index is 
1.1. In accordance with the District’s Regulation 2-5-301, TBACT is not required because each 
individual source risk does not exceed a cancer risk of 1.0 in a million and/or a chronic health 
index of 0.20. The estimated project cancer risk does not exceed 6.0 in a million and the project 
chronic health index does not exceed 1.0, but the project acute health index exceeds 1.0, so the 
project does not comply with the District’s Regulation 2-5-302 project risk requirements.  
 
To reduce the acute health index to no more than 1.0, the maximum hourly emissions of H 2S 
from S-110 must be limited to no more than 0.41 pounds during bypass operation. Modeling 
done to confirm compliance with Regulation 9-2-301 found that H 2S hourly emissions must be 
no more than 0.37 lb/hr during normal operation. Therefore, H 2S emissions will be limited to 
0.76 ppm:from A-110’s bypass stack, P-109). As described above in the Toxic Risk Screening Section, this
is expected to benefit air dispersion as the stack velocity at P-109 will increase compared to 
bypass mode operation for maintenance.
