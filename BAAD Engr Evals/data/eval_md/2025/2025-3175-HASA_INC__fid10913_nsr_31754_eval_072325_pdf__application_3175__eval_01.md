Hydrochloric acid (HCl) s olution containing ≤ 31.45% HCl, also known as muriatic acid, is received by 
tanker truck and unloaded into a storage tank .  At present, HASA has two muriatic acid storage tanks at the 
facility, one 10,000 -gallon tank (S -2) and one 6,000 -gallon tank.  The 6,000- gallon tank has never received 
a permit from the Air District and therefore will be included in this application as a new source, S -4.  
HASA plans to  replace the in -house scrubber currently abating S -2 with a new scrubber A -4.  Similar to S -
2, S-4 will be abated by a new scrubber A -4.  In the future, HASA plans to replace their 6,000 -gallon tank 
with a new 10,000- gallon tank.  Since HASA has decided to include the future 10,000- gallon tank in this 
application, the tank will be included in this evaluation report  and has been assigned source number S -6.  
HASA has stated that they will not operate S -4 and S -6 simultaneously as S -6 will be to replace S -4. 
 The HCl is  pumped from the storage tanks  to an indoor filling and bottling operation  (S-5).  At S -5, empty 
bottles of various sizes are placed on a conveyor belt that passes into the filler enclosure .  Aqueous HCl 
solution is supplied to the filler from the storage tanks .  Filled bottles leave the filler enclosure and 
conveyed into the final product loading area  enclosed by plastic curtains , where they are loaded on to 
pallets and into plastic crates for distribution .  The enclosed  filler  area is kept under negative pressure  and 
is vented to a  water scrubber .  The face velocity in the enclosed filler area is estimated to be 230 linea r feet 
per minute (lfpm).  Since the face velocity is greater than 100 lfpm , 100% capture  is assumed , con sistent 
with the recommendation from the Department of Industrial Relations on its web site at 
https://www.dir.ca.gov/title8/5154_1.html .  The in-house scrubber currently abating S -2 will be 
reconnected to S -5.

S-2 is currently subject to Permit Condition No. 21890 with a throughput limit of 1.3 million gallons per 
year of HCl.  The Air District’s Compliance and Enforcement Staff Betty Kwan conducted a compliance 
verification inspection at the site on April 20, 2022, and found that HASA had exceeded the throughput  
limit for S -2 since the beginning of 2021.  On April 24, 2022, HASA was issued a Notice of Violation 
(NOV #A61278) .  As a result of the NOV, HASA submit ted an application to the Air District to request an 
increase to the throughput limit for S -2, as it did not anticipate to ever be able to comply with the current 
limit of 1.3 million gallons per year .  Under this application, HASA is proposing to increase that limit to 5 
million gallons per year .  Because the emissions from S -2 will increase due to this new higher throughput 
limit, S -2 is considered a “modified” source, as defined in Regulation 2 -1-234. 
 
S-4, S-5, and S -6 require permits from the Air District because they will be used to handle and store up to 
31.45% HCl, which is above the permit exemption threshold of 30% by weight in concentration per 
Regulation 2- 1-123.2.4.  
 
The HCl emissions from the loading of the material are controlled by A -4 for the bulk storage tanks (S -2, 
S-4, and S -6), and A -1 for the bottling operation at S -5. 
 Because HASA has been operating its bottling operation (S -4) since  2011 and S -5 since  prior to 2018  
without a Permit to Operate, l ate fees and back fees were invoiced to HASA along with the standard 
application fees . 
 
This evaluation report will discuss the compliance of the proposed project , as described above , with all 
applicable rules and regulations.

EMISSIONS CALCULATIONS
ONS  
 
In December of 1999, US EPA issued a guidance for reporting HCl for purposes of compliance with 
Section 313 of the Emergency Planning and Community Right -to-Know Act (EPCRA ) of 1986 (link: 
https://ordspub.epa.gov/ords/guideme_ext/guideme_ext/guideme/file/hydrochloric%20acid.pdf ).  Per this 
guidance , EPA AP -42 document that presents models for estimating air emissions for organic liquid storage 
tanks may be used and adapted to estimate emissions of HCl  aerosols.   Therefore, the loading loss 
emissions of HCl in this evaluation report will be  estimated using the method in AP -42 Chapter 5.2 
(Transportation and Marketing of Petroleum Liquids ), and the breathing  loss emissions of HCl in this 
evaluation report will be estimated using  Breeze TankESP  software.  
 
The HCl emissions from this project will be reported under  Pollutant Code 1500, designated as 
“Hydrochloric acid mist pollutant ” in the Air District’s emissions database.  In the database, this particulate 
pollutant code is categorized under  the Pollutant Category of “Particulates” or TSP (rather than PM 10 or 
PM 2.5) due to the current lack of evidence that HCl mist droplets consistently fall below 10 micrometers in 
diameter.  Accordingly , it is appropriate to classif y these emissions  as TSP.  
 
Loading Loss Emissions of HCL  
 Emissions generated by transferring liquids into tanks, bottles, or other containers are called w orking loss 
or loading loss emissions and are calculated using the general equation below:  
Emissions = Loading Loss EF * Flowrate * Loading Points * (1- Control Efficiency)  
 
Loading Loss Emission Factor:  
The Loading Loss emission factor (EF) of HCl from storage tanks S -2, S-4, S-6 and bottling operation S -5 
is derived using the equation provided in AP -42, 5
th Edition, Chapter 5.2. This emission factor applies to a 
single loading point and is expressed in units of pounds of emissions per 1000 gallons of liquid loaded 
(lbs/1000 gal).  The derivation of this emission factor is explained in more detail below.  
Loading Loss EF = 12.46*S*P*M/T  = 0.45 lbs/1000 gal loaded per load point
