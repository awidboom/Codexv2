Background
ound 
Contra Costa County (“Applicant”) has applied for an Authority to Construct and Permit to Operate a new 
emergency stand-by diesel generator set. The proposed emergency generator is intended to provide backup 
power in case of a power outage to a new modular trailer unit at the County hospital located at 2500 Alhambra 
Avenue, Martinez, CA 94553. The trailer will house lab samples and possibly vaccines in specialized 
refrigerators. The refrigerators can’t lose power for a prolonged period of time without putting the samples at 
risk. 
 
New source: 
S- 3, Emergency Diesel Engine-Generator, Kohler Diesel Model KDI 3404TCR/70G, 2022 model year, 
Tier 3-certified, Engine Family NKHXL03.4TCF, 94 bhp, 70 kW

Regulation 6, Rule 1, “Particulate Matter – General Requirements” 
The proposed diesel engine S-13 is subject to the Section 6-1-310.1 limit on total suspended particulate 
emissions of 0.15 grains per dry standard cubic foot of exhaust gas volume. If it is assumed that the diesel 
engine exhaust gases contain 15% excess oxygen under normal operating conditions, this limit can be 
compared to the PM emission factors for the engine, as follows. 
 
From 40 CFR Part 60, Appendix A, Method 19, Table 19-1, a stoichiometric dry gas combustion factor of 
9,190 dscf/MMBtu is given for distillate oil combustion. At 15% excess oxygen, this factor becomes: 
(9,190 dscf/MMBtu) * (21%/(21%-15%)) = 32,165 dscf combustion products/MMBtu. The 
conversion of the Section 6-1-310 standard of 0.15 gr/dscf at 15% oxygen to lb/MMBtu is: 
(32,165 dscf/MMBtu) * (0.15 gr/dscf) * (lb/7000 gr) = 0.689 lb/MMBtu 
 
 Based on the fuel consumption rates for S-13, 0.445 MMBtu/hour, the certified PM emission rate from the 
engine in terms of fuel consumption is 0.01 lbs/MMBtu. Since this PM emission rate is well below the 
converted Section 6-1-310 emission rate of 0.689 lb/MMBtu, the engine complies with Section 6-1-310. 
 
Compliance with the Ringelmann No. 2 visible emission standard in Section 6-1-303, and the public 
nuisance prohibition in Section 6-1-305 is expected based on compliance with the Section 6-1-310.1 
emission standard and the certification of the proposed engine. 
 
Section 6-1-401 requires the operator to have the means to know the appearance of emissions from the 
operations at all times. Except during emergency operation, the engine will only be operated for 
maintenance and testing, and the operator is expected to monitor emissions during these manned hours of 
operation. 
 
Regulation 8, Rule 1, “Organic Compounds” 
Internal combustion engines are exempted from the provisions of Regulation 8 per Section 8-1- 110.2, so 
the organic emissions from the engine is not subject to any emission limit in Regulation 8. 
 
Regulation 9, Rule 1, “Inorganic Gaseous Pollutants – Sulfur Dioxide” 
Regulation 9, Rule 1 limits emissions of SO2 and applies to the emergency diesel engine.  Section 9-1-304 
limits the sulfur content of liquid fuel to 0.5% by weight. This standard will be met since California diesel 
fuel is limited to 0.0015% by weight. This low sulfur content is also expected to ensure compliance with 
the ground level concentration limit in Section 9-1-301. 
 
Regulation 9, Rule 8, “Inorganic Gaseous Pollutants – Nitrogen Oxides and Carbon Monoxide from 
Stationary Internal Combustion Engines” 
Regulation 9, Rule 8 applies to stationary internal combustion engines with a rated output greater than 50 
bhp. Section 9-8-110.5 exempts emergency standby engines from Sections 301 through 305, 501, and 503 
of this rule. Since S-13 will be operated as an emergency standby engine, the limitation to 50 hours of 
reliability-related operation in any calendar year in Section 9-8-330 applies. Compliance with this limit is 
expected. Operation during emergencies is not limited. In addition, the monitoring and recordkeeping 
requirements in Section 9-8-530 apply and are included in the standard permit conditions for the sources.

### General
Compliance Determination
Regulation 1, "General Provisions and Definitions" 
 
The facility is subject to Regulation 1, Section 301, which prohibits discharge of air contaminants resulting 
in public nuisance. The emergency diesel engine is a certified engine and will  be  used  for  limited hours, 
for testing and maintenance, other than during an actual 
 
 emergency. The engine is not expected to be a source of public nuisance, as the allowed hours of operation 
and emissions are low. 
 
Regulation 2, Rule 1, “Permits – General Requirements” 
California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA): District Regulation 2, Rule 1, Section 310 specifies that all 
proposed new and modified sources subject to District permit requirements  must be reviewed in accordance 
with CEQA requirements, except for ministerial projects or projects exempt from CEQA under Section 2-
1-312. The engineering review for this project requires only the application of standard permit conditions 
and standard emission factors in accordance with the District’s Permit Handbook, Chapter 2.3.1, Stationary  
Diesel  Engines. Since the proposed engine is subject to standard emission calculation procedures and 
standard permit conditions, this project is considered to be ministerial and is therefore exempt from  CEQA 
review. 
 
Public Notification: The public notification requirements of Regulation 2-1-412 apply to modifications 
which result in an increase in toxic air contaminant or hazardous air contaminant emission at facilities 
within 1,000 feet of the boundary of a K-12 school. The Applicant has reported a K-12 school within that 
radius of this facility and this was confirmed using Google Earth. 
 
The project is within 1,000 feet of Alhambra High School located at 150 E St, Martinez, CA 94553, and 
is therefore subject to the public notification requirements of Regulation 2-1-412. A public notice will be 
prepared and sent to all addresses within 1,000 feet of -13, Emergency Diesel Engine-Generator, and 
parents and guardians of students at Alhambra High School. All comments received will be summarized 
in this evaluation report. 
 
Regulation 2, Rule 2, “Permits – New Source Review” 
Best Available Control Technology (BACT): Per Regulation 2, Rule 2, Section 301 BACT is required of 
new or modified sources with potential emissions of 10.0 pounds per day or more of POC, NPOC, NOx, 
PM10, PM2.5, SO2, or CO. Maximum daily emissions have been calculated for S-13 based on continuous 
operation for 24 hours per day. Since the maximum  daily emissions from S-13 for 24 hours of operation 
does not exceed 10.0 pounds per day, BACT is  not triggered. 
 
POC and NOx Emission Offsets: The POC and NOx emission offset requirements are defined in District 
Regulation 2, Rule 2, Section 302. Under Section 2-2-302, POC and NOx emission offsets are required for 
new or modified sources at a facility which emits or will be permitted to emit 10 tons per year or more on 
a pollutant specific basis. Since the engine is a source of both POC and NOx emissions, evaluation of POC 
and NOx emission offsets is required. 
 
The determination of whether emission offsets are required is based on facility-wide potential emissions. 
A summary of the facility PTE can be found in Attachment 1. For the purposes of determining if emission 
offsets are required and for Title V/Major Facility review applicability, potential emissions from emergency 
engines are calculated in accordance with the District policy for emergency engines, dated 6/3/2019 (copy 
attached). The policy specifies inclusion of 100 hours of emergency operation in addition to the allowed 
discretionary operating hours for calculation of potential emissions. 
 
 The potential POC emissions from the facility including the potential emissions from the proposed new 
engine (0.55 tpy) will be less than the 10 tons per year POC emission offset trigger level, therefore POC 
offsets are not required for this project. 
 
Since the potential NOx emissions from the facility are less than 10 tons per year (8.07 tpy) NOx emission 
offsets are not required for this project. 
 
PM2.5, PM10, and SO2 Emission Offsets: The PM2.5, PM10, and SO2 emission offset requirements are 
specified in District Regulation 2, Rule 2, Section 303. PM2.5, PM10, and SO2 emission offsets must be 
provided for new or modified sources located at a facility with post- project potential emissions of 100 tons 
per year or more.  The post-project potential emissions  are 0.86 tpy PM2.5 and PM10 and 0.065 tpy SO2, 
less than 100 tons per year. Therefore, PM2.5, PM10, and SO2 emission offsets are not required for this 
project. 
 
Prevention of Significant Deterioration (PSD): The requirements in District Regulation 2, Rule  2, Section 
304 through 306 apply to PSD projects. A PSD project is defined in Section 2-2-224 and includes new or 
modified sources located at a facility that has potential emissions of 100  tons per year or more of any 
regulated NSR Pollutant (including fugitive emissions), if one of the 28 PSD source categories listed in 
section 169(l) of the federal Clean Air Act, or if not in a listed source category, 250 tons/year for each 
regulated air pollutant (excluding fugitive emissions for determining if a project is major). This facility is 
not one of the 28 listed PSD source categories, and the maximum potential facility-wide emissions will be 
less than 250 tons/year for each regulated air pollutant, therefore, this project is not a PSD project and is 
not subject to the PSD requirements in Sections 2-2-304 through 306. 
 
Section 2-2-307 applies to projects located in Class I areas; this project is not located in a Class I area, so 
this section does not apply. Section 2-2-308 applies to projects with a significant net emission increase in a 
pollutant subject to a National Ambient Air Quality Standard, as defined  in Sections 2-2-224.3 and 2-2-
227.2. The emissions from the proposed new emergency engine- generator is less than the significance 
thresholds in Section 2-2-227.2, so Section 2-2-308 does not apply. 
 
Public Notification of Notice and Opportunity for Public Comment: Per Regulation 2, Rule 2, Section 404, 
applications for a new major facility, major modification of an existing major facility, significant emission 
increases, and PSD projects are subject to public notice. A major facility for the purposes of this section is 
one which has potential emissions of 100 tons per year or more of any regulated air pollutant, excluding 
fugitive emissions. A significant emission increase is defined in Section 2-2-227.2 to include 25 tpy of total 
PM, 15 tpy of PM10, 10 tpy of PM2.5, 40 tpy or more of VOC. Since the emissions from S-13 will not be 
a significant increase the potential emissions for all regulated air pollutants is below 100 tons per year, and 
is not a PSD project, public notice under this section is not required. 
 
Additionally, the proposed source is not located within an Overburdened Community as defined in 
Regulation 2-1-243 and is not subject to the public notification requirements of Regulation 2- 1-412.
